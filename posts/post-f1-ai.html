F1 Meets AI: Predicting Who's Taking the Crown in Australian Grand Prix 2025 🏎️

Joel Yang
Security Engineer
Mar 10, 2025
4 min read

What happens when data science meets professional racing? In this project, I explored whether machine learning could predict the winner of the upcoming Australian Grand Prix. The results were fascinating — both for what the models got right, and what they couldn't account for.

The Dataset

I built a multi-faceted dataset that included historical race results, environmental conditions (weather, track temperature), and performance metrics like qualifying positions, lap times, and pit stop strategies. Feature engineering was crucial — creating derived features like "recent form" and "track-specific performance" made a significant difference.

The Models

Two primary machine learning approaches were tested:

  • Logistic Regression — a solid baseline for classification tasks.
  • Random Forest — an ensemble method that handles non-linear relationships well.

Both models were optimized using standard hyperparameter tuning techniques (GridSearchCV) and evaluated on accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score.

The Class Imbalance Problem

Here's where it got tricky. Both models achieved around 85% accuracy, which sounds impressive — until you realise the dataset is heavily skewed. A handful of dominant drivers win the majority of races, which means the model can get high accuracy by simply predicting the favourites. The precision for less frequent outcomes was notably poor.

"High accuracy doesn't always mean high usefulness. When your dataset is imbalanced, precision and recall tell the real story."

The Prediction

The AI ultimately favoured a dominant contemporary driver for the win — unsurprisingly, given the model's bias towards recent streaks and qualifying performance. But as any F1 fan knows, anything can happen on race day: safety cars, tyre degradation, and strategic gambles can flip the grid.

Key Takeaways

  • Feature engineering matters more than model complexity.
  • Larger, more balanced datasets are essential for reliability.
  • Real-world factors (weather, crashes, team strategy) can always override statistical predictions.
  • Sports analytics is a great domain for learning ML fundamentals.
