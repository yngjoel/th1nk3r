How a PCI DSS Audit Made Me Rethink Wazuh — and How You Can Be Audit-Ready Too

Joel Yang
Security Engineer
Nov 12, 2025
5 min read

When it comes to PCI DSS v4.0, one of the most heavily audited requirements is Requirement 10 — "Log and monitor all access to system components." Your SIEM is the backbone of compliance evidence. This guide walks through what I learned during a real PCI DSS audit — what worked, what didn't, and what specific evidence you can collect from Wazuh.

1. Confirm SIEM Coverage and Scope

PCI DSS Requirement: 10.3.4 — Ensure all in-scope systems are monitored. The first thing an auditor will ask: "Which systems are sending logs to your SIEM?"

CLI Evidence 
/var/ossec/bin/agent_control -l

This lists every connected agent and its status. Save the output as audit proof. Also check your agentless/syslog configuration:

/var/ossec/etc/ossec.conf 
<localfile>
  <log_format>syslog</log_format>
  <location>/var/log/nginx/access.log</location>
</localfile>

2. Show Log Collection and Event Details

PCI DSS Requirement: 10.2 & 10.3 — Track user activities and system events. Enable JSON archiving:

/var/ossec/etc/ossec.conf 
<global>
  <logall_json>yes</logall_json>
</global>
Restart manager 
systemctl restart wazuh-manager

3. Prove Time Synchronization

PCI DSS Requirement: 10.6.x — Ensure consistent time sources. Wazuh relies on system time:

NTP Check 
chronyc sources

Include /etc/chrony.conf from both the manager and agents to prove log alignment across all sources.

4. Demonstrate Log Retention and Integrity

PCI DSS Requirement: 10.5 & 10.7 — Protect and retain logs.

Archive Evidence 
ls -l /var/ossec/logs/archives/
lsattr /var/ossec/logs/archives/   # 'i' flag = tamper protection

5. File Integrity Monitoring (FIM)

PCI DSS Requirement: 11.5.1 — Detect unauthorized file changes.

/var/ossec/etc/ossec.conf 
<syscheck>
  <directories check_all="yes">/etc,/usr/bin,/var/www</directories>
  <realtime>yes</realtime>
</syscheck>

6. Access Control and RBAC

PCI DSS Requirement: 7.x & 8.x — Restrict system access and enforce authentication. Wazuh uses OpenSearch Security for role-based access control. Take screenshots of internal users, roles, and role mappings. Session timeout config:

/etc/wazuh-dashboard/opensearch_dashboards.yml 
opensearch_security.session.ttl: 900000    # 15 minutes
opensearch_security.cookie.ttl: 900000

7. Alerting and Notifications

PCI DSS Requirement: 10.4 & 12.10 — Review and respond to alerts.

/var/ossec/etc/ossec.conf 
<alerts>
  <log_alert_level>3</log_alert_level>
  <email_alert_level>10</email_alert_level>
  <email_to>security@yourdomain.com</email_to>
</alerts>

Organizing Your Evidence Pack

Each screenshot should include date, hostname, and navigation path for traceability. Recommended directory structure:

Evidence Directory Structure 
PCI_DSS_Evidence/
├── 01_SIEM_Coverage_and_Sources/
├── 02_Log_Collection_and_Event_Evidence/
├── 03_Time_Synchronization/
├── 04_Log_Retention_and_Integrity/
├── 05_File_Integrity_Monitoring/
├── 06_Access_Control_and_RBAC/
├── 07_Alerting_and_Notifications/
├── 08_Evidence_Tracker/
└── README.md

"Passing a PCI DSS audit for your SIEM isn't about paperwork — it's about evidence. Compliance stops being reactive and becomes a reflection of strong operational security."
